Describe initial efforts: Sarah Clark
We tried to track down any data that were collected based on funding from the EVOSTC, which requires making public any data 1 year after collection.
Steps to data collection and publication:
  1. Started with a list of projects: PI names and project numbers (some included project descriptions and most, years funded and associated agency)
  2. Searched for contact info of project PIs (google, agency sites)
  3. Where contact info was located, outreach emails were sent explaining the data recovery and archiving project and requesting data for the funded project(s)
    1. when possible, calls followed unreplied emails
  4. A reply to the email (or call) signified that we had accurate contact information and that the researcher was aware of our request for the data
    1. Replies gave us an indication of willingness to cooperate: “yes, I’ll get you that”, to “where do you get the right to ask me for data?!”
  5. once (if) data were sent, we worked with the data owners/custodians to format the data in persistent formats and create EML metadata
  6. Once researcher approves the data package, publish to public online database
Grouping and analysis since the initial effort: Jessica Couture
At the end of this 2 year effort, each EVOSTC project was characterized based on four descriptors: 1) final status of the project, 2) field of study, 3) agency sector or the main researching party, and 4) year the project ended. Status was classified using the project status labels we used during the efforts to track each step of the process [??? should I describe these here or will SC describe them above?] Field of study was determined based on title and abstract information (when available). Data are categorized into broad fields: physical, biological, oil, modeling. Since the majority of projects focus on biology, we divided these up further to look at different fields of biology as well. The main agency sector was based on them home institution of the project's primary investigator and grouped into projects lead by federal governmental, state/local governmental, academic, private - non-profit, private - for profit and tribal parties. Although projects were funded a year at a time, projects lasting multiple years were counted as the same project. EVOSTC grants require data to be published one year after collection but to avoid double counting, we based temporal analyses on the last year an entire time series of a project was funded.

Statistical Analysis:

For each of these four classifications, differences between groups were assessed using simple binomial statistics tests: Chi-squared and binomial linear regression (BLM). If the chi-squared test showed differences between groups, a BLM was run to identify which groups resulted in the biggest positive or negative result.
