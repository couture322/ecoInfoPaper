Introduction


In order to enhance discovery, efficiency, and transparency in their work, many scientists have been promoting a revolutionary approach known broadly as open science. This fledgling movement prioritizes the comprehensive, public sharing of scientific work, from initial planning through final publication and particularly data publication, as a means to enhance efficiency of the scientific community (Mascarelli 2009, Gewen 2013, Obama 2013, Hampton et al. 2015). Within the broader open science theory there is a strong focus on data sharing, highlighting that, when made publicly available, data can have a much higher impact than data that is limited to the creator’s analysis. Although this movement has been gaining steam and a number of tools have been designed to facilitate openness at each step of the scientific process are now available, those deeply involved in the development of open science acknowledge that the movement is still in its adolescence (Bjork 2004, Ram 2013, Hampton et al. 2015). Despite growing popularity for the theory of open science and data publication within scientific institutions around the globe, many researchers are discovering difficulties in the application of this exciting new technique (Costello et al. 2013, Van Noorden 2013, Hampton et al. 2015).
 
Accessibility of data, indispensable as a foundation of scientific inquiry, is an essential element within a more transparent science framework that faces both conceptual and practical obstacles. An historic culture of perceived ownership of one’s data, combined with an environment of competition for funding and access to publication space serves as a cultural impediment to the adoption of open data sharing practices (Hampton et al. 2015, Sieber 1989).  In an effort to defend intellectual novelty and secure publication opportunities, scientists often withhold data from the larger scientific community.  In addition, sharing data was difficult in the past, and while digitization has helped, it has not eliminated hurdles to sharing data publicly.  Many commonly used data practices and tools neglect to prepare data for publication or sharing and therefore require additional training, effort, and resources to complete this step.  Better tools to help scientists responsibly archive their data in a manner that will be accessible and preserved through time are available and continue to be developed (Hampton et al. 2015, Michener & Jones 2012, Ram 2013, Baker 2017). But these tools will only be effective when scientists change their data practices and understanding of the value of long-term archiving their data.  
 
Increasing support for the open science movement would suggest an improved willingness to share data and underscore the hypothesis that more data should be available in recent years than in earlier history. William Michener et al. hypothesize a temporal degradation in the knowledge of one’s own data, concluding that the older data are the less information exists about the data and associated metadata both within the collector’s memory and the physical form of the data. Recent studies have supported this hypothesis (Michener 1997, Vines et al. 2014, Baker 2017). The assumption that older data will inherently be less accessible than newer data even to the creator is further compounded by an increased availability of data documentation and sharing tools one would expect an increase in data availability over time. Larger and faster servers, more accessible tools such as Github, cloud services and free online repositories, all of which have been popularized in the past decade, should lead to more data sharing overall than in previous decades (Hampton et al. 2015, Ram 2013, Reichman et al. 2011). 
 
In addition to variation in data retention and accessibility over time, differences in data sharing may occur between research fields or sectors. Differences in data collection protocols, innovations in instrumentation, or confidentiality of information between research fields may lead to better data preservation in some disciplines. For example, data collected automatically via advanced instruments and can usually be downloaded automatically with pre-defined analysis protocols. Scientists producing this sort of data may be more open to data sharing since the collection process is automated and it requires little to no additional work to share the data.  Alternatively, confidential data such as that involving human subjects as used in the social sciences, could present many hurdles to sharing that might inhibit data sharing and preservation. Similar to potential differences between research fields, a scientist’s agency affiliation may influence willingness or ability to share data. A private consulting agency may prefer to keep data private in order to protect client confidentiality or increase profitability.  In contrast, if a scientist collects data under a public agency, their department may compel or even require data publication (Obama 2013). Many public government agencies, have both external and internal data sharing policies, and are more likely to provide established protocols and systems of data sharing for their employees.
 
A number of studies have tested the rate of data reporting from published articles and usually focused on one journal in particular and found low recovery rates ranging from 10-30% (Wolins 1962, Wicherts et al. 2006, Savage & Vickers 2009, Alsheikh-Ali et al. 2011, Vines et al. 2014). Journal enforced data publication requirements might be perceived as more of a burden than a responsibility since there is no inherent change of ownership of the data with the acceptance of a manuscript, and therefore data creators will likely maintain protective attitudes towards their data although signing off on the requirements in order to publish. When funders require data publication, researchers might feel more obliged to provide data since the requirements were in place prior to data creation and therefor data ownership is shared with (or held by) the funder. In this case, since the “reward” has already been given, data sharing might be considered more of a moral obligation than an ambition-driven step, as might be expected when submitting for journal publication. To test whether funding requirements result in different rates of data sharing than journal requirements, we wanted to determine if the recovery rate for a specific funding agency would differ from the results of journal specific data salvage efforts.
 
Here we assess the ability to retroactively archive ecological and environmental data and evaluate patterns in recovery of a funding body. To test these trends, we focused our study on the data-collection efforts of the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council (EVOSTC).  The EVOSTC, instituted in 1989 to manage monetary damages by Exxon Mobile following the Exxon Valdez oil spill in the Gulf of Alaska in 1989, has funded hundreds of projects since its inception. The EVOSTC requires the publication of data within one year of data collection for all recipients of their grants, but do not specify archiving methods nor provide a specific publication platform. The EVOSTC did make an effort to collect these data in the mid 1990s but the success of this effort is unknown as the content of this collection has since been lost.  EVOSTC grants have funded an array of government entities, private consulting firms and non-governmental organizations, as well as a few Alaskan native groups. The diversity of the grantees was also compounded by the variety of scientific disciplines under which they operated.  We wanted to know 1) for how many of these projects we could recover data? 2) Were there trends in data reporting based on data or grantee characteristics? 3) If data were not procured, why we were unsuccessful?
