Methods

Data recovery and archiving:

From 2012 to 2014, a team of on full-time staff and three student interns was assigned to collect and archive data funded by the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council (EVOSTC) targeting those funded between 1989 and 2010. Project information was obtained from the projects page on the EVOSTC website which includes varying levels of detail for each project, ranging from project title only to full bibliographic information and attached reports. We tracked the progress the data request and acquisition process for each project based on six stages, which are outlined as follows: “emailed”, “replied”, “sent data”, “data revised”, “published”, “unrecoverable”. Contact information was obtained through agency sites and Google searches based on the information we were able to gather from the EVOSTC site. If we were able to find contact information for the listed principal investigator (either email or phone number), and outreach email or phone call was made explaining the data recovery project and requesting data for the project in question. Projects for which outreach could be made were labeled “emailed”. If we then received a reply to the outreach and could therefore confirm that the contact information was correct, the project label was promoted to “replied”, regardless of level of cooperation expressed in the response. Once we received data from for a project, the label was changed to “data sent”. It was often difficult to determine how much data were collected for each project so if any data were received, the project was deemed successful. If the data were clean enough and/or the contact was responsive enough to guide us through cleaning, formatting and metadata creation the data were labeled “data formatted”. Once the contact approved our edits and compiled metadata, the products were then published to the Gulf of Alaska Historic Data Portal and the project was labeled, “published”.

Statistical Analysis:

At the close of the data collation effort, we plotted the number of projects that fell into each of the above status labels in order to visualize the overall status of our archiving mission.

Results of the outreach efforts are further characterized based on three project descriptors: research field of the project (field), agency sector of the home institution of the principal investigator (sector), age of data when data when recovery efforts were initiated (age). Age of data is calculated as the number of years since the last year a project received EVOSTC funding since some projects spanned multiple years.

The impacts of these characteristics on the likelihood of providing data are assessed using simple binomial statistical tests: Chi-squared and binomial linear regression (BLM). Chi-squared tests were run on each group of characteristics individually to test for differences in success of obtaining data within a group. A BLM was run on all of the data to test which elements of the projects are most indicative of data reporting across all groups.

Hurdles to recovery:

Also of interest, are the reasons data were no recovered. To quantify these responses we categorized the projects for which we were unable to gather any data based on notes from our correspondences.  The projects included in this analysis include those labeled “emailed”, “replied” and “unrecoverable” in the status categorization. Projects in the category “replied” where instances where we were able to confirm contact information of the contact and send a data request but lost communication before the data was sent. These projects have been re-categorized here as “communication lost”. Of the “unrecoverable” projects, notes were used to divide these into: no contact information, data lost, non-digital data, unwilling to share, and requested additional funding. The projects from the “emailed” status have been added to the “no contact information” group since we were never able to confirm the outreach efforts were received due to no reply from the target recipient. Data are only labeled “data lost” if our contact confirmed that they were so. Similarly, the non-digital format, unwillingness to share and funding requests are only labeled as such for projects were data requests were rejected for these confirmed reasons in communications. Non-digital data is deemed “unrecoverable” here since our project lacked the resources to convert or store such data. We have since converted these data, when possible, and refer to these data in the discussion.
