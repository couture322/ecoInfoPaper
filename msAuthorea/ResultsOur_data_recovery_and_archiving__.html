Results

Our data recovery and archiving success

        This effort to recover and archive data from research funded by the EVOS Trustee Council spanned 2 years, and involved a team of 4 people (1 full-time staff, 3 part-time student interns).  We were able to publish data from 60 of the 315 projects funded between 1989 - 2010 (Figure 1).  Though we were only able to recover and publish data from 26% of the projects, we wanted to know what increased our likelihood of recovering data, so we examined whether the research field or sector influenced our recovery of project data.  We recovered data from 58% of physical oceanography projects, 38% of oil projects, 10% of social science projects, and 0% of modeling projects, but these research fields had less than 30 projects each (n = 19, 29, 21,11, respectively) (Figure 2).  Biological projects were the most numerous (n = 235), yet we only recovered data from 24% of those projects (Figure 2).  To understand this pattern, we divided the biological field further and found the highest recovery rate (45%) for invertebrate-focused projects (n = 20), and the lowest recovery for habitat-focused (19%, n = 27) and fisheries projects (20%) despite fisheries studies being the most numerous (n = 94).  Other projects focused on birds, mammals, and plankton (n = 58, 33, 3, respectively) had recovery rates in between those extremes (24%, 27%, 33%, respectively) (Figure 2).  Research sectors generally did not affect our data recovery success significantly, but interesting patterns indicate that "privately" collected data may increase the change of data recovery compared to data in the public trust at governmental agencies (Figure 3).  We also examined the effect of the time elapsed since the end of the project, and found average recovery rates of ????
