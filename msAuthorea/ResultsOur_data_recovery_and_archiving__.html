What factors increased our likelihood of recovering/archiving data ?

      Though we were only able to recover data from 26% of the projects, we wanted to know what increased our likelihood of recovering data.  We examined the effect of the time elapsed since the end of the project, and found average recovery rates of ???? 20% ???? and 70% ???? for studies that ended >5 five years and <5 years before recovery efforts respectively (Figure 4).  However, other factors are likely influencing this seeming increase in recovery for recent projects (see Discussion). 
      We also examined whether the research field or sector influenced our recovery of project data.  While we recovered data from approx. 60% of physical oceanography projects, 35% of chemical oceanography projects, 15% of ecosystem/habitat projects, and 10% of social science projects (Figure 5), these research fields had no more than 36 projects each.  Biological projects were the most numerous (209), yet we only recovered data from 25% of those projects (Figure 5).  We broke down the biological field even further and found the highest recovery rate (45%) for invertebrate-focused projects, and the lowest recovery for fisheries projects (19%) despite fisheries studies being the most numerous (95).  Research sectors generally did not affect our data recovery success significantly, but interesting patterns indicate that "privately" collected data may increase the change of data recovery compared to data in the public trust at governmental agencies (Figure 6). Overall, if projects were in the physical oceanography field and conducted by a federal government agency, we were more likely to recover data from these projects.
