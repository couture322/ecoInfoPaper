Results

Data recovery and archiving success
        We were able to obtain data from 60 of the 315 projects funded between 1989 - 2010 (Figure 1).  Though we were only able to recover and publish data from 26% of the projects, we wanted to know what increased our likelihood of recovering data, so we examined whether the research field or sector influenced our recovery of project data.  While we recovered data from 58% of physical oceanography projects, 38% of oil projects, 10% of social science projects, and 0% of modeling projects, these research fields had less than 30 projects each. Biological projects were the most numerous (235), yet we only recovered data from 24% of those projects (Figure 2).  To understand this pattern, we divided the biological field further and found the highest recovery rate (45%) for invertebrate-focused projects, and the lowest recovery for habitat-focused (19%) and fisheries projects (20%) despite fisheries studies being the most numerous (n = 94). The home sector of the PI was not found to be statistically significant (p = 0.7604) (SuppFigure 1).  We also examined the whether the recovery of data was correlated with the time elapsed since the end of the project, and found that recovery averaged 27% over the 23 year period, but overall there was no significant effect of time on recovery (p = 0.08) (SuppFigure 2).  However, other factors may be influencing temporal trends in data recovery (see Discussion).  Overall, if projects were in the physical oceanography, oil, plankton or benthic invertebrate fields we were more likely to recover the data regardless of how long ago a study was conducted.  

The results from the random forest analysis support the results of the chi-square tests, determining the research field as the most important variable in determining if a project was successfully archived. This analysis further identifies benthic invertebrates, plankton, oil and physical oceanography data as the classifications that determine successful data acquisition, while bird, fish, habitat, modeling, mammal and social data were most likely to indicate unsuccessful acquisition (Figure 3). No sector or temporal categories were listed as important indicators, further supporting their lack of significance.

Hurdles to data recovery
      To examine why our efforts did not recover more data, we examined the frequencies of interruptions in the data recovery process. Loss of communication was the largest hurdle to recovering data.  The majority of projects for which data were not recovered became stalled after some initial contact with the Principal Investigators (PIs) had been made, but contact was ultimately lost before any data were recovered (50%, n = 116) (Figure 4).  While this could have been because PIs had reasons they wouldn't share the data (see Disucssion), these reasons were not communicated and PIs specifically requested funding for recovering only 1% (n = 3) of non-recovered projects and flatly refused to share data from only 2% (n = 4) of non-recovered projects.  The second most common hurdle to data recovery was a lack of contact information for PIs or data managers, including projects entirely lacking contact info, as well as those with phone numbers or e-mail addresses from which we never received a reply (35%, n = 82) (Figure 4).  Data was entirely lost from 7% (n = 17) of the non-recovered projects due to deceased PIs, obsolete technology, and discarded files. We found the frequency of non-digital project data to be quite low (4%, n = 10) and not one of the main factors in our failure to recover data (Figure 4). Overall, the biggest hurdle to recovering data remains a loss of communication with the data producers or PIs at all stages of the recovery process.
